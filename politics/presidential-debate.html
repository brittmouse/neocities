On The Harris / Trump Debate

September 17, 2024

Note: Quotes are taken from the transcript of the debate here. Emphasis added is mine.

A Brief Intro

At the time I'm writing this, the United States is in the final two months of the 2024 presidential election, and we are a week out from our first (maybe only) debate between the two main presidential candidates. Vice President Kamala Harris (D-CA) and former president Donald Trump (R-FL) both went to Philadelphia to make their case as to why they should be president of the United States and not their opponent. My mind was made up well before this but I still wanted to judge how well Harris would do in a debate against someone who has usually looked strong on the stage, as well as get a look into her actual policies into how she would handle climate change, energy independence (and whether that's even the best solution), foreign policy, and restraining large corporations.

What I got instead was a little policy, a lot of sniping at Trump from Harris, and a lot of Trump stabbing himself in the dick. And some of the best laughs I've had this year.

Performance

I was anxious ahead of the debate over how well Harris would do against Trump. He's a very belligerent debater that likes dragging opponents. Clinton fell for it hard in 2016, and Biden was a victim of this in the previous presidential debate before he dropped out. But Harris surprisingly didn't. She was nervous starting off and immediately irked me by pivoting to a rehearsed stump speech instead of answering a basic question about whether Americans were better off today than they were 4 years ago (generally yes but it's complicated). Despite that start, she got much better as the night went on, landing some good hits on Trump's ego but still refraining from any strong policy statements.

Not like she needed to with how Trump acted. Much of it was his old standard rambling, whining, and lying, but there were some egregious failures. He was asked whether he had a plan on replacing the Affordable Care Act and replied "I have concepts of a plan." He went on a strange rant about how Haitian immigrants in Springfield, Ohio were "eating the dogs [...] they're eating the cats, they're eating – they're eating the pets of the people that live there." He said Harris "wants to do transgender operations on illegal aliens that are in prison." He was especially bizarre.

It was even harder to get any policy positions out of Trump because he kept bringing it back to fear-mongering over illegal immigration (not like Republicans want Latin Americans or Africans / Afro-Caribbeans coming in legally anyways). You might be able to tease out some policy positions but it was delivered so strangely that it was nearly impossible to make it out. It just didn't make much sense.

Harris really got him on his ego though. She got several digs in at him over the size and attendance of his rallies, how he only got his real estate empire because of his father's wealth and not form any talent on his part, and how he'd been fired by 81 million people. The petulant, angry way he responded to each of these, wasting valuable time on talking about how big his rallies are instead of actual policy (or just ignoring the topic at hand to fear-monger over the millions of "illegals" pouring in). Harris used his ego perfectly and was able to use his response to deflect from the times she refused to give any policy specifics or dodge hard questions.

Policy, Policy, Policy: What is it Good For?

Both candidates positioned themselves as continuations of their past. Trump made reference over and over again to how good "his" economy was, how America was strong on the world stage, and how he was going to clamp down further on the border. Harris instead brought up some of the good things the Biden administration has accomplished over the past 3-and-a-half years, like the increased child tax credit, investing one trillion into clean energy, and supporting Ukraine with military aid.

However, when pushed on the negatives of their policies, or on their inconsistency, both dodged in their own ways. Trump would talk about how the numbers were faked or how it was fantastic, actually. Harris usually either dodged into a stump speech or tried to spin it in a good way. That may have been for the best as a debate strategy, as it saved her from having to explain complicated topics like supply-chain logistics, monetary policy, and the concepts of deterrence and strategic ambiguity to the most foolish demographic G-d ever created: the median voter. Getting a lecture in political science or economics is not something that wins over voters; vibes and "looking right" gets more median voters. However, there are questions I believe you should own and answer honestly.

Economics: The Worst Social Science

Harris didn't give a good answer on whether Americans were better off four years ago than they were now. I think we are, but "better" and "good" are two different ideas. Four years ago, we were in the midst of the COVID pandemic and protests over police brutality and racism. Four years ago we were at the peak of the second rise of COVID cases and deaths, averaging over 100k cases per day1 and nearly 2000 deaths per day2. Four years ago (September 2020) the unemployment rate was 7.8 percent3. In many ways the economy is better, but better is not good, and "good" is defined by the median voter as "I can afford groceries and my rent / mortgage." And that has been far more difficult for the past several years for many reasons, including supply chain squeezes leaving years-long knots in the system (especially when dealing with the nightmare that is the international shipping industry), increased consumer demand, changing fiscal habits from work from home (citation needed), and corporate greed. It's difficult to explain all this to voters because a) it's very technical and doesn't make for a good story, and b) the median voter doesn't give a damn about "Congressional deadlock" or "forces outside the White House's control", they think the president can just do anything.

Trump played off that ignorance when she did pitch her policy positions. He responded multiple times to Harris' policies with "she's been vice president for three-and-a-half years and she hasn't done it yet." Her policies on the economy are decent but lacking. She wants a $6000 child tax credit, a $4000/family raise compared to current levels, and a $50k tax deduction to new small businesses. She said some vague things about bringing down prices on housing and food but there was very little in the specifics. Despite these incredibly vague promises, Trump both said "She's going to my philosophy now" and "She's a Marxist" within the same paragraph.

Trump used those attacks to play up his flat 20% tariff on every good we import. If you'll remember, Trump implemented heavy tariffs during his term that interfered heavily with free trade with our neighbors and with the larger global economy. This lead to retaliatory tariffs being places on U.S. goods, including China placing a large tariff on American agricultural products, cutting the revenue from American agricultural trade to China in half4. The general economy also suffered, with higher prices being passed onto American consumers 5 6. Having a flat tariff on every single import would directly translate to higher prices for consumers, since countries exporting goods to the U.S. would pass that cost off to importers, and importers would pass that cost off onto consumers.

Energy

Harris supports a move away from fossil fuels but still takes the national security / economic protectionist stance of continuing to produce fossil fuels to become a net exporter of oil and gas. This makes sense from a national security and economic standpoint as oil is a valuable commodity that can fuel civilian and military production. Making America a net exporter might allow us to join the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries (OPEC), giving us a lot more influence over global oil prices and competing with Russia and Saudi Arabia in an arena where we've usually been the target, not a dealmaker.

This would also mean continued profits for petro-barons and further commitment to a system of energy production that we know is causing climate change. She didn't even mention alternative / clean energy sources during her answer; she simply stated "I won't ban fracking" and "We need to invest in diverse sources of energy."

Trump blamed Harris for causing high oil prices, something that I really doubt anyone in the government can really mess with much. It is funny that when prices go up it's the government, and when prices go down it's the economy. He said he'd do way more fracking and said solar was bad but that he also liked solar.

I'm disappointed with Harris' answer. Only mentioning "diverse sources of energy" once feels like a major snub of environmentalist groups and to everyone dead from last year's major natural disasters. I expected bad from Trump but I'm more annoyed at Harris. Perhaps she was just keeping that quiet as the question was about whether her values had changed over whether fracking was good and not what her energy policy was specifically, but I'm still annoyed.

Foreign Policy

When asked if she believed she bore any responsibly for the chaotic withdrawal out of Afghanistan, Harris said "I agreed with President Biden's decision to pull out of Afghanistan." That certainly is not a simple "yes" or "no," but saying "I agreed with the decision" does suggest you take responsibility for the consequences of that decision. It isn't decisive but it wasn't Biden's "they [the Afghan National Army] did not have the resolve to fight,"7 which in my opinion completely abdicates responsibility. Harris did use the question to criticize Trump for bypassing the government of Afghanistan entirely to negotiate directly with the Taliban, including inviting them to Camp David. If Obama had done that, it would have taken the entire 1st Armored Division to keep people from burning down the White House.

Harris reiterated her support for Israel, for a ceasefire and hostage deal (something neither party of the Israel-Hamas war seems interested in, especially Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu), and for a two-state solution, which may be entirely out of the question now. She also reiterated her support for Ukraine's war against Russian aggression, without saying whether she would be in favor of letting Ukraine hit military targets deeper inside Russia with American munitions. It was the normal liberal answer I would expect from a boring Democrat.

Trump meanwhile claimed these wars never would have happened if he was president (1. specious reasoning, 2. bullshit) due to how strong he is. He has this weird idea that just by looking tough, he can get other countries to do what he wants, which worked really well to deter Iran from getting nuclear weapons and funding international terrorism. I call this the "whip your dick out to assert dominance" school of international relations theory. Works great, right until you drive a hammer into it, as strong, stupid men frequently do.

(I'll have to write some about the state of American foreign policy analysis in the future, but in the meantime here's Sarcasmitron's excellent video on the topic.)

Trump's answer on Ukraine was bad. When asked "Do you want Ukraine to win this war?" he responded "I want this war to end." Insisting that Ukraine negotiate with Russia without mentioning any specifics essentially means that Ukraine should accept at least giving up Crimea, probably also Luhansk and Donetsk Oblasts, and potentially Zaporizhzhya and Kherson Oblasts. Calling for "negotiation" when the U.S. is not the country being invaded or bombed suggests either personal interest in Russia dominating and controlling eastern Ukraine or a general disregard for the well-being of those residing outside your borders, either of which Trump and Vance could fit.

I'm less interested in the China stuff. They both tried to out-strong the other and none of it was that convincing. China would be less of a threat if "anti-war" movements like the LaRouchians would stop convincing everyone they're already at war.

Post-Debate Ramifications

Springfield, Ohio has been reeling from the past week of racist and exterminationist rhetoric. Republicans are openly lying and completely fine with it. There have been bomb threats called to schools and government buildings. The migrants have been called "cockroaches," rhetoric ripped right from the start of the Rwandan Genocide. Republican politicians are calling for anywhere from 11 to 25 million people to be deported, a population transfer that would dwarf the massive humanitarian disasters in Syria, the Democratic Republic of the Congo, and Sudan. It would require a surveillance and logistical network dwarfing the state Hitler created to hunt down Jews, Romani, and Slavs.

Harris had a pretty good night and a lot of energy behind her campaign. I think her recent interview with the National Association of Black Journalists was a big letdown. She again showed she isn't willing to answer the actual questions that voters have by throwing around numbers like they mean anything instead of giving us a narrative to rally people around. She isn't even talking about raising the minimum wage! It's depressing and insulting that she gives so little effort to answering questions people actually have.

My choice was made up years ago. Republicans were and still are exterminationist when it comes to trans and gay people, non-white people, and political opponents. Jill Stein of the Green Party can't call Putin a war criminal and doesn't know how many voting members of the House of Representatives there are. I disagree with the Party for Socialism and Liberation on nearly every single foreign policy position they have (long live the State Department). I'm voting for Harris.

But it was absolutely worth watching that debate. Aside from Trump's incredible gaffs and Harris' great performance, it was at least worth it to remind myself how nuts the former president is and how easy it is to insult him. I felt sane for a little bit. Granted, an entire week has passed and about a year's worth of news occurred so I'm no longer mentally sound, but it was nice while it lasted.
